Color Features:
Dominant Yellow and Red Hues: Ripe mangoes typically have a vibrant yellow color with hints of red or pink blush. You can extract RGB values or use color histograms to capture the higher intensity in the yellow and red channels.

Reduced Green Intensity: Compared to unripe mangoes, ripe ones have a lower intensity in the green channel. Color segmentation or thresholding can help identify this shift from green to yellow/red.

Texture Features:
Smooth but Slightly Wrinkled Surface: Ripe mangoes may show faint wrinkles or slight unevenness on the skin. This can be captured using texture analysis techniques, such as Local Binary Patterns (LBP) or edge detection, to identify small, shallow lines.

Softness Indicators: While softness is usually not directly visible, small skin deformations or tiny wrinkles near the stem can indicate ripeness.

Shape Features:
Fuller, Rounded Shape: Ripe mangoes often have a plumper appearance. Contour analysis or calculating the aspect ratio may capture this shape.

Slightly Oval or Rounded Contours: Most ripe mangoes have rounded, smooth contours. Shape descriptors like roundness or circularity can help quantify this feature.

Color Features:
Dominant Green Color: Unripe mangoes have a predominant green color, with minimal to no yellow or red. You can extract color histograms or mean RGB values to represent this feature.

Lower Red and Yellow Intensity: Compared to ripe mangoes, unripe ones have significantly lower values in the red and yellow channels, which can be used as distinguishing features.

Texture Features:
Smooth Surface: The skin of unripe mangoes is smooth and taut, with no visible wrinkles. Texture analysis techniques like Gabor filters or Local Binary Patterns (LBP) can help capture this smoothness.

Softness Indicators: While softness is usually not directly visible, small skin deformations or tiny wrinkles near the stem can indicate ripeness.

Shape Features:
Angular Edges Near Stem: Unripe mangoes may have slightly sharper or more defined edges around the stem area. Contour analysis and shape descriptors (like aspect ratio and roundness) could be used to identify any subtle angularity.

Slightly Flatter Shape: Some unripe mangoes have a less plump or full shape. Aspect ratio or contour shape analysis may capture this feature, depending on the mango variety.

Scientific name (fungus causing it): Colletotrichum gloeosporioides

Casuse/s:
The anthracnose is mainly caused by the fungus Colletotrichum gloeosporioides. It is caused in rainy climates and the fungus thrives in wet conditions.

The disease produces leaf spots; blossom blight, twig blight, and fruit rot symptoms. Tender shoots and foliage are affected which ultimately cause “die back” of young branches.

Treatment:
For treatment, you need to apply fungicide before the expected rainy season. You also need to prune its branches for proper air circulation. Other than that, you need to stop watering the plant from overhead.

Trees can be sprayed twice with Bavistin (0.1%) at 15 days intervals during flowering to control blossom infection. Spraying of copper fungicides about 0.3% is recommended for the control of foliar infection

Scientific name (fungus causing it): Aspergillus

Casuse/s:
A post harvest disease and it's caused by Aspergillus niger Van Tiegh. Affected fruits show yellowing with irregular grayish color spots, which develops into a black necrotic area with the growth of black mold.

Treatment:
The black mold rot disease can be managed by avoidance of injury to Mango fruits and its contact to soil, dipping of fruits in hot water with Carbendzim (0.5%) for 5 minutes and covering of fruits with brown or newspaper bags on the tree itself 15 days before harvest.

Scientific name (fungus causing it): Lasiodiplodia theobromae

Casuse/s:
During the harvesting of mangoes avoid any damage to the mangoes because then it makes it easy for pathogens to enter the fruit. You also need full post-harvest fungicide treatment to prevent it from happening and spreading.

Water stress during fruit development can predispose fruit to infection, manage irrigation, and root rot control carefully. pre -harvest sprays of fungicides to control bacterial black spot or anthracnose can reduce the incidence of stem-end rot in fruit. Prune trees to improve ventilation and spray.

Avoid harvesting immature fruit and cool fruit immediately after harvest and store in well-ventilated containers.
